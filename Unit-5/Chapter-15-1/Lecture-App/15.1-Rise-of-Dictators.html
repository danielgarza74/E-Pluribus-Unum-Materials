Introduction

The interwar period was marked by profound fragility. The scars of World War I, combined with the global economic devastation of the Great Depression, created a vacuum of power and hope. In this environment, democratic institutions faltered, and radical strongmen emerged across Europe and Asia. These dictators promised national resurrection, economic stability, and strict social order—but at the cost of absolute obedience and the systematic destruction of individual liberties. This lecture examines the structural roots of this instability and the catastrophic diplomatic failures that led the world back to the brink of global conflict.

Scenario 1

A nation is crippled by hyperinflation and 30% unemployment. A charismatic leader holds massive rallies, blaming ethnic minorities and foreign powers for the country's "humiliation," and promises a 1,000-year era of national glory.

Scenario 2

An international organization issues a formal "reprimand" after a major power invades a resource-rich neighboring territory. However, the organization has no military and refuses to impose meaningful trade sanctions, allowing the invasion to stand.

Scenario 3

A prime minister returns from a high-stakes diplomatic meeting, waving a signed paper and declaring he has secured "peace for our time" by allowing a dictator to keep territory he already invaded, hoping the dictator's hunger is satisfied.

Scenario 4

A military force utilizes coordinated radio communication, fast-moving armored tank divisions, and precision dive-bombers to overwhelm a border defense in days, bypassing heavy stationary fortifications entirely.

Scenario 5

A government takes absolute control over all newspapers, schools, and radio stations. Every citizen is required to display the state's symbol, and a secret police force arrests anyone suspected of "disloyalty" to the state's vision.
